**Reflection (book report):**

I really liked it. It was funny in places, and I think I will come back to this article AFTER I finish my "12 weeks" of training to be interview ready. Right now a lot of the concepts start to get lost on me, but it is genuinely interesting to see the perspective of someone so well versed in interviewing people for the position I want, and even more so for the position I want in the future.

Breadth vs. depth interviews is a concept I had not heard named before, though I have felt it in my own experience. I have had to interview people who are very strong in one specific area but lack the necessary breadth, and this framework sounds like it helps you recognize a candidate's strengths and weaknesses so you can fill out a team accordingly.

Two points stood out to me. First: "Finally, having a basic understanding of evals is key for anyone building ML-powered products." Second: "Science application interviews challenge a candidate to apply their skills and knowledge to a practical problem." Together these tell me that, even though the work is practical, you still have to retain the knowledge you have studied. Put more succinctly, it is about showing what you know. I understand the importance of completing projects and solving real problems even more now.

I had never heard of the AICE framework (ambiguity, influence, complexity, and execution). Although it is largely related to soft skills, the way each element is broken out really helped me understand why each one matters, not just for an ML engineer but for any role I might take:

- Ambiguity: being able to solve a problem whatever state it arrives in, whether fully specified and detailed, missing notes or requirements, or the classic "we have an idea, now go build it."
- Influence: HOW you create impact, defined as the scope of your collaboration plus your impact on people. You can write a Confluence page explaining a process, build a tool, solve the problem directly, or raise awareness over email. What matters, as the author says, is becoming a force multiplier rather than measuring yourself by some number of PRs a day.
- Complexity: this refers to the problem itself, not the solution, since the goal is always an elegant solution. It can also describe how many moving parts something has.
- Execution: "the ability to deliver within limited resources and timelines." That sounds like the whole of it, but it also lives within the cycle of innovation and failure.

Those were the key points that stuck with me. If this is what hiring managers look for, I will try to mold myself into someone with these traits, like the astronaut Jose Hernandez: if they needed a diver, I would become a diver; if they needed an engineer, I would become an engineer.
