- Bias vs. activation function, these are separate mechanisms, and I initially conflated them. Bias (`+ b` in `Wx + b`) shifts a linear function so it doesn't have to pass through the origin (e.g. a food-delivery-time model needing a nonzero minimum time even at distance 0). Bias works fine with zero activation functions; stacking multiple purely linear layers, each with its own bias, is still algebraically just one linear function with a combined bias (`W2(W1x + b1) + b2 = (W2W1)x + (W2b1 + b2)`). What activation functions (ReLU, etc.) actually gate is whether *depth* (stacking layers) buys anything at all: without a non-linearity between layers, a 10-layer network has the same representational power as a 1-layer network, it can only ever fit a straight line/plane. With non-linear activations, stacked layers can approximate curves and complex decision boundaries.
- Tensor broadcasting rule, restated correctly after a first imprecise pass: shapes are compared dimension-by-dimension, aligned from the trailing (rightmost) dimension; two dimensions are compatible if they're equal or one of them is `1`; if one tensor has fewer dimensions, the missing leading ones are treated as `1`. Concretely: `(3,2)` broadcasts against `(2,)` (trailing dims 2 and 2 match) but NOT against `(3,)` (trailing dims 2 vs. 3, neither is 1, no alignment possible) — it's per-dimension and right-aligned, not "the whole tensor just needs one side to be size 1."
- What a tensor gives you over a plain Python list, autograd is the load-bearing one, not GPU speed or ecosystem convenience. `backward()` computes ∂loss/∂parameter for every parameter via the chain rule (reverse-mode autodiff / backpropagation), which is exactly what gradient descent needs to know which direction and how much to adjust each weight. GPU acceleration and `torch.nn` integration make training practical and fast, but without gradient tracking, none of that would let you actually train a model, you'd have no mechanism to compute weight updates at all.
